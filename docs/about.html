The person with the idea

Ideas are easy.
Making them real
is the work.

Who

Markus Reuter
aka roitsch.

I work at Deutsche Telekom — at the T Gallery in Bonn. My job is to explore possible futures. Not by collecting trends. Not by filling Miro boards or writing strategy papers that live in a shared drive and shape nothing. By building future scenarios in real life — so people can walk into them, experience them physically, and form an actual opinion about what they want.

The T Gallery is a space where ideas stop being abstract. Where a future mobility concept becomes a room you can walk through. Where a vision of how we might work or live or communicate is something you touch, not something you read about in a PDF.

"The gap between idea and experience is where most futures die. The work is collapsing that gap."

That instinct — make it real, make it experienceable, don't let it exist only on a slide — runs through everything. It is why Third Wave Wichteln existed. It is why BrewLog exists.

The thread

The same instinct,
three times.

01
T Gallery · Deutsche Telekom · Bonn
Building futures, not describing them

When a future scenario becomes a room people walk through rather than a document they read, something fundamentally different happens to their understanding of it. That's the premise the T Gallery is built on.

02
2013 – 2019 · with André Krüger & Tho Keller
Third Wave Wichteln

An international coffee gift exchange that connected more than 2,000 people across 50+ countries through a bag of coffee and a handwritten note. It started as a simple idea — what if specialty coffee people sent each other what they loved — and became something that ran for six years across five continents. It ended when COVID stopped the logistics and the trust-based system showed its limits. But it existed. That matters. thirdwavewichteln.com ↗

03
2025 · three evenings · €20
BrewLog

A tool I wanted and couldn't find. Built not because the idea seemed feasible, but because not building it felt like a waste. The feedback loop — scan, recommend, brew, log, learn — exists now. It runs on my phone. It gets smarter with every cup.

The parallel

The same instinct,
every time.

What changed is the barrier. Building BrewLog — with a back-end, a database, an AI recommendation pipeline, authentication, a taste profile engine — used to require a team, a budget, months. Now it requires being able to describe clearly what you want and having the patience to refine it. The tools do the execution.

That shift matters far beyond coffee apps. When the cost of making an idea real collapses, the ideas worth making real change completely. You stop filtering for what is feasible and start building what is actually useful — to you, precisely, without compromise.

"The extraordinary thing is not that I built something sophisticated. It is that I was able to build exactly what I wanted — not a compromise, not a product shaped for a market, but something calibrated entirely to one person's way of working."
