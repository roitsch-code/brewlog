Why it exists

An article, a befriended
coffee expert,
and a missing tool.

The spark

An AI sommelier
gets something right.

It started with Mateusz Petlinski sharing an article in the New York Times about an AI wine sommelier. Mateusz has a reliable talent for sending me down rabbit holes. More than a decade ago he was the person who made me understand how specialty coffee actually works. Before him I was brewing an espresso blend as filter and calling it a day. Today I travel through airport security with a precision kit that makes the staff genuinely unsure whether I am a chemist or something worse.

The AI sommelier thesis in the article was correct — and the implications go further than just getting a recommendation. Feed a model enough honest personal data and it becomes a confident, calibrated interlocutor. Not just for deciding what to drink, but for knowing what to ask the human sommelier, for articulating your own taste more precisely, for having a conversation about flavour with actual substance behind it.

"What if the data from all those careful brews could teach something back to you — patterns in what you actually love, not just what you think or say you'd love?"

The problem

The execution
was wrong.

But how most people use AI for taste and recommendations is almost worthless. A one-off question, no personal context, no history — the model has nothing to work with beyond generalised knowledge. It tells you what most people with your stated preferences might like. That is not the same thing as what you will like.

A great sommelier carries years of expertise and empathic understanding. But even they cannot connect the dots across hundreds of your cups. They don't know what you drank last Tuesday, in what mood, with what food, brewed how. They can't know that your palate shifted in winter, or that you rate a certain origin lower when you brew it before 8am, or that one roaster you'd written off was consistently suffering from your own over-extraction rather than their sourcing.

That gap is not filled by expertise alone. It is filled by data — your data, accumulated over time, fed back honestly into a model that has the context to interpret it.

The tools that existed were either passive archives (great for logging, useless for learning) or generic recommendation engines (optimised for a broad audience, not for one person). Nothing closed the loop. Nothing took what you carefully documented and made it actionable for the next cup.

Brew diaries

✗
Generic recommendation apps

Built for an audience. Average preferences, average results. The opposite of personal.

✗
One-off AI queries

No history, no context. Like asking a stranger on the street what you should order for lunch.

✓
BrewLog

Every session feeds the next. The loop is the product. Documentation and recommendation are the same tool.

The insight

Data occasions,
not just features.

The design principle behind BrewLog is not "add useful features." It is: create more reasons to generate honest data, so the recommendations get sharper over time.

Every interaction is a data occasion. A home brew gives you the full loop — recipe, execution, result, attribution. A coffee shop visit gives you a taste log without the brewing variables. The Match Finder gives you a pre-purchase evaluation against your actual history. The Explore page gives you a place to build knowledge and ask questions in context. Every one of these occasions teaches the system something.
