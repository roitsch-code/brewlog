Why it exists

An article, a befriended
coffee expert, and a gap.

It started with a friend sharing an article in the New York Times about an AI wine sommelier. The article was about using AI as a one-time shortcut — scan a wine list, get a recommendation. Useful, maybe. But it's also the weakest version of the idea. A single prompt without context, without history, without any of the data that makes a recommendation meaningful. The hypothesis worth testing is a different one: what happens when the model has everything you've ever brewed, rated, and noted?

The gap was obvious. No tool for coffee built that loop. Brew diaries were passive archives. Recommendation apps were built for everyone, which means they were built for no one in particular. Nothing took what you carefully documented and made it actionable for the next cup.

How it works

Scan, brew, log.
Repeat until it knows you.

Every session starts with a bag scan — Claude reads the label and fills in what the label doesn't say. Then four taps set the context: occasion, amount, time available, taste mood. The model returns an exact recipe tuned to the coffee's character, your equipment, and your actual brew history. Grind size to the degree. Pour sequence as cumulative weights.

After brewing, you log the result: rating, flavour notes, body, acidity. And two dimensions most apps ignore — how well it was executed, and whether it was your style regardless of execution. Each data point flows back into the next recommendation.

The loop works at home with your V60 and in a coffee shop across town. Every interaction — a home brew, a café visit, a bag you scan before buying — is a data occasion that makes the whole system sharper.

Fork it

Open source.
Adapt it in an afternoon.

The entire personality of the recommendation engine lives in two files — the system prompt with the equipment list and taste baseline, and the match scoring logic. Swap those for your own setup and it becomes your tool, not someone else's. The stack is Next.js, Firebase, and the Claude API. It runs as a PWA on your iPhone.
